Start of the War

World War II began on September 1, 1939, with Germany's invasion of Poland, followed by the Soviet invasion of Poland from the east on September 17, in accordance with the non-aggression pact between the two countries (the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact). The Polish invasion resulted in the division of the country between the Nazis and the Soviets.

In response, the United Kingdom and France declared war on Germany on September 3, 1939. From that point onward, the conflict quickly expanded into a global confrontation.

The Expansion of the Axis

In the early years of the war, the Axis powers (Germany, Italy, and Japan) achieved a series of military victories. In 1940, Germany invaded Denmark, Norway, France, the Netherlands, Belgium, and Luxembourg. The blitzkrieg (lightning war) used by the Nazis was highly effective, allowing them to quickly conquer large areas of Europe.

On June 22, 1941, Germany launched Operation Barbarossa, the invasion of the Soviet Union, the largest military attack in history. At the same time, Japan expanded its presence in Asia by invading China and, in 1941, attacking the United States at Pearl Harbor, which led the U.S. to officially enter the war.

The Role of the Soviet Union and the United States

In 1941, the Soviet Union entered the conflict after being invaded by Germany, while the United States joined the Allies after the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor. The war became truly global, with battles taking place in Europe, Africa, Asia, and Oceania.

The Soviet Union faced a brutal battle on the Eastern Front, especially in the Battle of Stalingrad (1942-1943), which marked a decisive turning point in favor of the Allies and resulted in a major defeat for the Nazis. Meanwhile, the United States helped finance the war and provided resources to the Allies, in addition to fighting in key battles in the Pacific and Europe.

The Turning Point of the War

The turning point in the war occurred in 1942 and 1943. The German defeat at Stalingrad and the Allied victory at the Battle of El Alamein in North Africa marked the beginning of the Axis's downfall. In 1944, the Allies carried out D-Day, the invasion of Normandy, which opened a second front against the Nazis in Western Europe.

In the Pacific, after the decisive Battle of Midway (1942), the United States turned the tide against Japan, advancing to retake Pacific islands and, eventually, dropping atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945.

The End of the War in Europe and Asia

In May 1945, with the capture of Berlin by Soviet forces and the unconditional surrender of Germany, World War II in Europe came to an end. Adolf Hitler committed suicide on April 30, 1945, as Allied troops advanced through Berlin.

The German surrender was signed on May 7, 1945, but the war in Europe was effectively over.

In the Pacific, the war continued after Germany's surrender. The United States increased pressure on Japan, which refused to surrender. In August 1945, the U.S. dropped atomic bombs on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, forcing Japan to surrender on September 2, 1945, marking the end of World War II.

Consequences of the War

World War II caused global devastation, with over 70 million dead, millions more wounded, and vast material destruction. The conflict resulted in the redrawing of the global political map, with the division of Germany and the start of the Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union.
